## 1. What shipped (plain language)
In a mirror room, you now exist in both halves of the board at once: two black squares, one per half, driven by one set of controls — up/down move both the same way, left/right move them as mirror images. When a wall blocks one body, the other still moves, and knocking the two out of step is how these rooms are solved. Folding banks a *pair* of grey echoes (one per half) that can hold switches on both sides of the divide at once, and you win only when **both** bodies stand on their own exits on the same turn. Five new rooms teach this — lockstep, desync, cross-half holds, patrols, and the oversized room 35 "Reflection", which is now the end of the campaign. All of it lives in a new engine and new screens beside the old ones, so nothing about rooms 1–30 changed.

## 3. Decisions I made during this phase
1. **A parallel `MirrorRoomView` (clean branch in `ContentView`) instead of a `RoomEngine` protocol.** The brief offered either; the protocol would have made `RoomView` generic over two engines — touching the verified single-body screen. The parallel screen leaves `RoomView` byte-for-byte untouched at the cost of a small deliberate duplication (the HUD/win-overlay shell). Covered by D-075's consequences — **no new entry**.
2. **The engine exposes its own prediction surface (`plan(for:) -> MirrorStepPlan` + public `bodyCollides`), and the view reads it instead of re-implementing movement.** Direct response to the Phase-4.03 lesson (the view/engine landing divergence an adversarial review caught): there is deliberately no view-side copy of the mirror rules to drift. Implementation detail within D-075 — **no new entry**.
3. **`Direction.horizontallyMirrored` is an extension inside `MirrorGameState.swift`** — the reflected-controls rule in code without modifying `Direction.swift` (out of scope). **No new entry**.
4. **`MirrorEcho` is a named struct (id + `left`/`right` `Echo`s), not a tuple**, so SwiftUI can identify the pair and the two grey bodies animate via the member echoes' own ids. **No new entry**.
5. **Two `BoardEffectsOverlay` instances (one per half)** render the fold peel and death dissolve at each body's own tiles — reusing `BoardEffects` untouched rather than extending its descriptors for two-body events. A fold plays two ripples/peels (one per start); a death plays two dissolves ("both bodies dissolve"). First-pass, Design-refinable. **No new entry**.
6. **Death-overlay echo matching stays land-on only** (as `BoardView`'s is). In mirror the echo cross-paths branch is *live* (desync breaks parity), so a swap-death dissolves both bodies correctly but omits the swapping echo-body's glide-in cameo. Documented in the view header and current-state; presentation-only. **No new entry** (flagged for the Design pass).
7. **Two additive `BoardMetrics` tokens for the divide** (`divideWidth` 2 / `divideOpacity` 0.30) — the 4.03 pad-token precedent for first-pass marks; ink, monochrome, never the accent. **No new entry**.
8. **Room 33's doors are both cross-half AND-doors (`heldBy: [sA, sB]`).** The first draft (each door held by the *other* half's single switch) had a genuine 1-fold cheese the Python BFS found: the live right body could *transiently* do sB's job at the door, then catch up through the echo-held dR. The AND form kills the line (whichever body leaves its switch last still faces a closed door) and matches the D-077 intent's "and/or an AND-door spanning halves". Caught and fixed **before** shipping, so it is authoring iteration, not a post-hoc adjustment — **no new decision id** (the D-038/D-039 precedent is cited for the method).
9. **Audio for a desynced turn voices the blocked body's `.stay` as the calm tick** in the step chord (one tick per body's actual step + echo-body steps) — desync is audible, honest to "every action paired with feedback". **No new entry**.
10. **Tap input maps a tap adjacent to *either* body** (left body → the direction itself; right body → its mirror), since one input drives both. Swipes use left-body semantics. **No new entry** (first-pass input; device feel will judge).
11. **Room budgets within the intent ranges:** 32 ships at budget 1 (intent "1–2") and 34 at budget 2 (intent "2–3") — the tight end in both cases, cheese-checked. **No new entry** (inside D-077's stated ranges).

## 7. Risks, follow-ups, what the next phase needs to know
- **Device debt is at its peak.** Three Part-4 bands (wait, teleport, mirror) have never rendered on a real screen, and mirror is the most novel to feel. Play rooms 21–35 on the iPhone **before** Phase 4.07 defines mirror × teleport — room 36 stacks both.
- **The mirror visuals are a deliberate first pass** (the divide rule, the two-body read, two ripples per fold, two dissolves per death, the swap-death cameo gap in §3.6). A Design pass may re-specify them; the engine surface (`plan`/`bodyCollides`/positions) is what it should drive.
- **For 4.07 (mirror × teleport):** `MirrorGameState` deliberately has no pads — every walk uses `Echo.position`'s empty-pads default. The interaction spec will decide where `resolveLanding` enters the two-body walk (per body? shared?); the engine's `plan(for:)` is the single place movement resolves, which should localise the change.
- **Mirror authoring rules learned (for room 36):** the echo cross-paths branch is live (desync breaks parity); a first recording on a board whose blocks are all mirror-symmetric can only rest on mirror-aligned pairs (lockstep invariant); reflected controls preserve the bodies' column-sum except at one-sided horizontal blocks — both invariants are useful for proving rooms un-cheesable; and banked echoes never block, so they expand nothing (only doors they hold do). The turn-1 rule generalises: every banked echo body's turn-1 cell must leave the live pair an escape in *each* half.
- **The suite is the finale's safety net:** 203 methods / 3321 assertions pin every rule and all 35 rooms. Run it after any engine or level change.
